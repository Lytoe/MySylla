About

I’m a linguistics/NLP student in Paris trying to turn code and coffee into language tools that actually work. I build clean web interfaces, keep my architectures strict, and break things just fast enough to fix them again.

I grew up in Iran, studied English Didactics for my bachelor’s, and spent years teaching languages — which means I’ve heard every possible grammar mistake twice and still somehow enjoy it. That teaching background now leaks into my code: I obsess over making interfaces as clear as a good classroom explanation.

France is a paradox I live inside: I admire the art, the language, the streets that look like cinema—yet every paper, queue, and prefecture visit feels like punishment for existing. I came here to study and build, not to wrestle with endless forms and stamps. Still, the chaos sharpens me. Paris is the place where I code, curse, and somehow grow.

Iran is the weight I carry and the fire that made me. The mountains, the poetry, the stubborn humor in the face of limits—it all shaped me. But every layer of beauty was wrapped in restrictions that kept tightening. I learned early how to bend rules, how to fight quietly, how to dream beyond the walls. Leaving felt like betrayal and survival at the same time; Iran is the home I argue with in my sleep.

These days I’m deep into computational linguistics and my main project: a 15k-line web app that throws LLMs, TTS, and custom NLP pipelines into one Clean Architecture blender. It’s messy, ambitious, and the closest thing I’ve built to a second brain.

When I’m not coding, I’m out with a camera, butchering French idioms, or writing half-serious thoughts about tech and learning. It keeps me sane and reminds me that technology should feel more like a creative outlet than a corporate treadmill.

“Code is just grammar with worse exceptions.”

— my linguistics and coding philosophy
